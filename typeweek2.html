Week 2 Review
Typography

This Week, I’ve Learned …

One aspect that has become very clear to me in two short weeks is that with consistent practice and the right direction, one can learn to become very comfortable with the basics of typography quite quickly. An upfront disclaimer, this is not to say that mastering or even learning typography is easy, and I am not fat enough into my journey to make a fair comment just yet but like anything you practice, the mystery seems to disappear and gets broken into smaller, more digestible chunks.


It’s All Relative

Something that Typography 101 really drives home is the importance of how the elements of a given design relate to one another. It’s funny, while Chris goes through some sample exercises and critiques some beginner typography, one example that he calls out is that some designers take text and arbitrarily shove it into the corner of the page (or screen) in an attempt to create whitespace. I ALWAYS did this, thinking I resembled some Swiss genius because my design seemed (at the time) clean and crisp with plenty of breathing room. This one point really hit home and once you learn how to group elements and get them to relate in some way, it avoids the mistake of making arbitrary design decisions and sticking things in corners because you are scared to put them anywhere else!

Practice Makes Perfect

No matter how much you consume content in the form of reading, watching, listening or otherwise, you have to put in that road work! Nothing, and I mean nothing, beats practice. After watching all of the content on the Typography 101 course and reading from the greats such as Massimo Vignelli and Josef Müller-Brockmann, I just knew that the homework assignments given by Chris would be a breeze. I had consumed so much content that I already felt like I was way ahead of what I had originally expected. Fuck that!. I stumbled and fumbled my way through the first exercise and felt both frustrated and stupid. This seemingly easy exercise drove me nuts and took me way outside of my comfort zone. But funnily enough, with each day of practice that passed, and this practice was every day without exception, I began to get less frustrated and began to grow in confidence. Before I knew it, I could approach the dreaded blank page knowing at the very least, how to approach the problem. Along with this, I have continued to trace from the masters which gives the added benefit of observing how the greats would approach and solve a design problem.

4. Follow Along Training.

In Typography 101, there are recordings where Chris critiques a designers work on a Helvetica case study. Instead of passively watching this critique (which alone, was very interesting), I also followed along to recreate the case study, applying the improvement areas mentioned by Chris.

5. DIY Homework.

This was the most difficult part of my week. As part of Typography 101, Chris gives a 7 week homework assignment adopted from Art Center and the Basel School of design which challenges the student to design a magazine cover given some specific constraints. This weeks constraints where to design 10 variations of magazine cover using 1 typeface, one point size and one weight. I thought that I would breeze through this but the experience was frustrating, humbling and ultimately, very rewarding. It forces you to stick to the basics and not apply any ‘tricks’. It is time quite consuming and requires a considerable amount of effort but from just a weeks experience, is probably the most important part of the module. It forces you to create something without tracing or watching some else do it.

In Closing.

I am delighted with how the first week has gone and feel much more comfortable working with the dreaded type than I had been before. Next week will be much of the same activities with some slight variations
